MAE (Mean Absolute Error)
- definition:
  Average of absolute differences between actual and predicted values.
  MAE = (1/n) * Σ |y_true - y_pred|

- why to use:
  To measure average magnitude of errors in a simple, interpretable way.

- when to use:
  When all errors should be treated equally (no extra penalty for large errors).

- advantage:
  Easy to understand (same units as target)
  Not sensitive to outliers

- disadvantage:
  Does not penalize large errors strongly
  Less useful when big mistakes are critical


MSE (Mean Squared Error)
- definition:
  Average of squared differences between actual and predicted values.
  MSE = (1/n) * Σ (y_true - y_pred)^2

- why to use:
  To heavily penalize large errors and make model sensitive to outliers.

- when to use:
  When large errors are very costly and should be minimized strongly.

- advantage:
  Strong penalty for large errors
  Smooth and differentiable (good for optimization)

- disadvantage:
  Sensitive to outliers
  Units are squared (harder to interpret)


RMSE (Root Mean Squared Error)
- definition:
  Square root of MSE.
  RMSE = sqrt(MSE)

- why to use:
  To get error in same unit as target while still penalizing large errors.

- when to use:
  When you want interpretability + sensitivity to large errors.

- advantage:
  Same unit as target variable
  Penalizes large errors

- disadvantage:
  Still sensitive to outliers
  Slightly harder to compute than MAE


R² Score (Coefficient of Determination)
- definition:
  Measures proportion of variance explained by the model.
  R² = 1 - (SS_res / SS_tot)

- why to use:
  To understand how well the model explains data variability.

- when to use:
  When comparing models on same dataset or evaluating fit quality.

- advantage:
  Scale-independent
  Easy to interpret (closer to 1 is better)

- disadvantage:
  Can be misleading with non-linear models
  Does not indicate prediction error magnitude
  Can increase with irrelevant features


Adjusted R² Score
- definition:
  Modified R² that penalizes adding unnecessary features.
  Adjusted R² = 1 - [(1 - R²)(n - 1)/(n - p - 1)]

- why to use:
  To evaluate models with different numbers of features fairly.

- when to use:
  In multiple regression when feature count varies.

- advantage:
  Penalizes overfitting
  Better for model comparison than R²

- disadvantage:
  Still not a direct error metric
  Harder to interpret than R²
